## What Went Well

### Breakthroughs worth replicating

1. **Story 5.6 pinned the v1.1 GDPR event taxonomy *before* Epic 6 implemented it.** Contract test `PartyDomainEventPublicationContractTests` (and the 5.6-added entries) locked the names `PartyMerged`, `PartyErased`, consent-recorded, consent-revoked, processing-restricted, and processing-restriction-lifted in the Contracts assembly. Epic 6 then landed and consumed those names verbatim with zero rename pressure. This extends the Epic 1 / 2 / 4 same-epic taxonomy lock to a **cross-epic forward commit**: when the current epic owns the contract assembly that downstream epics will publish from, lock the *future* taxonomy *in the contract assembly itself*, not in a planning document. Verified in production code by Epic 6 closure.

2. **Documentation guardrail tests promoted as a first-class pattern (Stories 5.4, 5.5, 5.6, 5.7).** Instead of "we wrote some markdown," the markdown is *asserted against* by tests in `tests/Hexalith.Parties.Sample.Tests` (`PartyErasedHandlerPatternTests`, `SampleOnboardingGuardrailTests`, `TolerantDeserializationTests`, etc.). Story 5.4 pins per-broker ordering text for Redis Streams, RabbitMQ, Kafka, and Azure Service Bus. Story 5.5 pins privacy-minimal read-model guidance. Story 5.7 pins the MVP soft-deactivation vs v1.1 erasure distinction and the EventStore boundary link. This is the Epic 4 "promote policy to executable fitness" lesson applied to *documentation* rather than to *code boundaries* — same shape, new surface. If a future contributor deletes the documented prose in a doc cleanup, the suite fails.

3. **Story 5.1 reached the published-events boundary with the Epic 1 rejection-taxonomy lock + Epic 1.8 log-safe domain model intact.** Successful state-change events and `IRejectionEvent` payloads remain distinguishable in the *published* event stream (not just in the aggregate's internal handling). The publication contract assembly does not leak the rejection-vs-success distinction as prose — it lives in the type system. Lessons compounded without cross-cutting rework.

4. **Story 5.2 proved envelope `ToString()` redacts payloads, test-side.** The log-safety claim is asserted, not trusted. Same shape as the Epic 1.8 personal-data fitness test, reaching the event-envelope surface. Envelope sequence numbers are also asserted ordered for multi-event party mutations — a foundation that Story 5.4's sample handler then exploits via the highest-processed-sequence-per-aggregate skip pattern.

5. **Story 5.3 extended the Epic 3 deployment-validation pattern to pub/sub.** Missing `pubsub*.yaml` components are now a structural deploy-time blocker — not a runtime surprise. Same shape as the Epic 3 wildcard-app-id and wildcard-path guards, applied to a new failure mode. The `tests/Hexalith.Parties.DeployValidation.Tests` suite now covers missing-pubsub, missing-dead-letter-config, subscriber-in-publishing-scopes, wildcard-app-id, and missing-component-resiliency-targets — five independent deploy-time guards on the same boundary.

6. **Story 5.4's sample handler is a working idempotency proof, not just guidance.** Tracking highest-processed `sequenceNumber` per aggregate and skipping replays with different CloudEvents ids but identical sequence is implemented and tested (55 tests). The "consumer must be idempotent" claim is therefore proven by a worked example with a test suite, not asserted by prose alone. Out-of-order older updates do not overwrite newer local read-model state.

7. **Story 5.7 maintained the MVP-vs-v1.1 distinction as a structurally-visible response shape.** `PartyDeactivated` is documented as active/inactive business state, *not* as legal erasure or subscriber cleanup signal. Future `PartyErased` payload guidance limits cleanup metadata to party id, tenant id, and erasure timestamp — *no personal data*. This is the Epic 4 Story 4.6 pattern (`operation = soft-deactivation`, `gdprErasurePerformed = false` as first-class fields) extended to the subscriber-side event guidance. Consumers reading the docs cannot accidentally treat MVP deactivation as GDPR erasure cleanup.

8. **Forward-compat tolerant deserialization is asserted, not assumed (Story 5.6).** `TolerantDeserializationTests` covers the case where a consumer receives an additive future field on `PartyMerged`. The "additive evolution" claim in the architecture is therefore proven for the actual serialization stack used by consumers, not just argued by contract review.

The blast radius of an accidental refactor of that fixture is wider than the Epic 4 boundary lock. Addressed by **B15** (separate `PartyForwardCompatEventContractTests` fixture).

## Significant Discoveries (transferable design principles)

**Epic update required: NO** (Epic 6 already validated the forward-compat taxonomy in production code; Epic 7 / 8 plans unaffected). The discoveries below are transferable principles, not amendments.

1. **Cross-epic forward taxonomy commit is a distinct pattern from same-epic taxonomy lock.** Epic 1 / 2 / 4 each locked their *own* epic's taxonomy in story 1. Story 5.6 locked the *next* epic's taxonomy in the *current* epic's contract assembly, because Epic 5 is the structural author of the Contracts package and Epic 6 publishes from it. The bet held when Epic 6 landed without rename. Generalize: when an epic owns a contract assembly that downstream epics will publish from, lock the *future* taxonomy *in the contract assembly itself*, not in a planning document. The contract assembly survives epic boundaries; planning documents drift.

2. **Documentation guardrail tests are the documentation-side equivalent of executable architectural fitness.** Stories 5.4 / 5.5 / 5.6 / 5.7 assert against documented prose via tests in `tests/Hexalith.Parties.Sample.Tests`. If a future contributor deletes the per-broker ordering note, the privacy-minimalism guidance, or the MVP-vs-v1.1 erasure distinction in a routine doc cleanup, the suite fails. This is the only mechanism that survives contributors who did not attend the retrospective and never opened the story file. Worth canonizing as a first-class pattern for any story labeled `non-feature preparation`, `documentation`, or `external-consumer guidance`. Captured as **B14**.

3. **Renumbering collisions are now observable in the filesystem, not just in retro filenames.** Pre-renumbering `5-1-mcp-server-setup-and-get-party-find-parties-tools.md` coexists with current `5-1-publish-stable-party-domain-events.md` under the same directory. Sprint-status disambiguates by full story key, but raw `5-*.md` glob mixes two epochs. Epic 4 B9 anticipated this for retros and old stories; Epic 5 now provides the live confirmation. B9 action remains valid and is now demonstrably necessary — not hypothetical.
