# Consumer-Trust & Regulated-Language Review — parties

## Overall verdict

The spine's voice intent is genuinely good — "say what will happen, then name the right" is the correct posture, and the plain-language rules in EXPERIENCE.md are mostly honoured in the mocks. But the consumer privacy screen makes two promises the system probably cannot keep (a hard 30-day erasure SLA and a sub-minute export), frames cancellable erasure without ever stating that *completed* erasure is irreversible, and presents a lawful-basis list whose three different bases (contract / consent / legitimate interest) imply more user control than the law actually grants. These are trust-and-legal exposures, not cosmetics: fix the promises and the basis honesty before this copy ships.

## Findings

- **[critical]** Hard 30-day SLA committed in user-facing copy. EXPERIENCE.md Voice-and-Tone Do-column ("It'll be gone within 30 days") and `consumer-privacy.html` State B banner ("It'll be gone within 30 days.") both promise a fixed completion window. GDPR Art. 12(3) explicitly allows extending the one-month period by two further months for complex/numerous requests, and an event-sourced crypto-shred propagating across projections is exactly the kind of operation that can run long or stall. A confident "within 30 days" that slips becomes a documented broken promise to a data subject. *Fix:* commit to the **start of the obligation**, not a guaranteed finish: "We've started erasing your data. We aim to complete this within 30 days and will confirm when it's done." Reserve any exact date for a per-request status the backend can actually compute.

- **[critical]** Irreversibility of *completed* erasure is never stated; only cancellability is. `consumer-privacy.html` State B shows only the upside ("You can change your mind any time before it completes" + a plain "Cancel erasure request" button). Crypto-shredding is irreversible the instant the key is destroyed — and "before it completes" is a moving, possibly-short boundary, not the comfortable 30-day runway the banner's framing implies. A user who reads "any time within 30 days" and tries to cancel on day 20, only to find it already shredded, experiences both data loss and a broken promise. *Fix:* state both halves honestly — "You can cancel while this is in progress. Once erasure completes, it's permanent and can't be undone." Do not let the 30-day number and the cancel window read as the same window.

- **[high]** Lawful-basis list implies user control that doesn't exist for two of three bases. `consumer-privacy.html` State A "What we hold about you" lists *contract* (Name & contact), *consent* (Email preferences), and *legitimate interest* (Sign-in history) in identical visual rows, directly above a consent section with toggles. Only the consent-based item is withdrawable as a toggle; you cannot "withdraw" a contract or a legitimate-interest basis the way you flip consent. The undifferentiated layout invites the user to expect a switch on all three (and to feel deceived when contract/legitimate-interest data persists after they "turned everything off"). *Fix:* visually and verbally separate "things you control" (consent toggles) from "things we must keep to run your account / by law" (contract, legitimate interest), and for the latter name the right that *does* apply — object (Art. 21) for legitimate interest, not "withdraw." One honest clause: "Kept to provide your account — you can't switch this off while your account is active."

- **[high]** Export latency over-promised in both spine and flow. EXPERIENCE.md Flow 1 ("ready in a moment", "in under a minute, from his couch") and `consumer-privacy.html` set an expectation a server-side portability job over an eventually-consistent read side cannot reliably meet. When it takes two minutes or needs an email link, the product looks broken against its own copy. *Fix:* drop the time commitment from the spine; the toast should say "Preparing your export — this can take a little while. We'll let you know the moment it's ready," and keep the synchronous-download path as the *happy* case, not the promised baseline. Add format/portability expectation while here ("a machine-readable file (JSON) you can keep or move").

- **[medium]** Default-On marketing toggle + softening copy is a mild opt-out asymmetry. `consumer-privacy.html` State A ships "Marketing emails" defaulting **On** with the sub-label "On — occasional offers," while "Product updates" is Off. Consent under GDPR must be opt-*in* (Art. 7 / Recital 32) — a pre-checked marketing consent is not valid consent, and "occasional offers" is gentle confirm-shaping toward keeping it on. *Fix:* consent toggles default **Off** until the user grants; mirror the neutrality of the withdraw/grant copy ("On — you'll get occasional offers" / "Off — you won't get marketing emails") without editorializing the value of staying on.

- **[medium]** "Request erasure" / "erasure" is residual legalese for an anxious consumer. EXPERIENCE.md's own rule is "no legalese." "Erasure" is the regulation's word, not the plain word; an anxious user scanning on a phone reads "delete." The danger-outline styling is appropriate caution (not over-discouraging — outline, not fill, is the right weight for a *reversible-until-complete* action), but the label could be plainer. *Fix:* lead with the human verb and name the right in support: button "Delete my data," with the right named nearby ("This is your right to erasure (right to be forgotten)."). Keep the danger-outline weight.

- **[medium]** Conflicting/duplicated status signals on the same screen. `consumer-profile.html` Edit state shows simultaneously a green toast "Saved — updating…" *and* a freshness line "Saving your changes…" (info dot) — two different tenses of the same operation at once (saved vs. saving), which undercuts the "looked done the instant he acted" confidence the flow is selling. `consumer-privacy.html` State B pairs the green "Saved — updating…" toast (a positive, command-accepted color) with an *erasure* request banner — a success-green toast for "we're deleting your data" is a jarring mismatch. *Fix:* one status source per action; for erasure use the neutral/info treatment, not success-green, and don't show "Saved" and "Saving" together.

- **[low]** Profile email value differs between view and edit, suggesting silent data drift. `consumer-profile.html` view shows `marc@example.com`; the Edit screen's prefilled value is `marc.delacroix@example.com`. In a mock this is a copy slip, but on a consumer data-accuracy screen it reads as "the system shows me one thing and is about to save another." *Fix:* keep the rendered value identical to the stored value across view↔edit; only diverge when the user actually types.

- **[low]** PII-in-copy: spec is correct, watch two surfaces. The erasure tombstone copy ("This party was erased." / "Scheduled for erasure," no fields) and the freshness/toast strings correctly carry **no** names or ids — consistent with the never-log-PII rule. Two places to hold the line in implementation: (a) the Admin typed-confirmation ("type the person's name") and (b) any toast/notification that interpolates a name. *Fix:* keep all consumer toasts/banners/tombstones PII-free as specified; ensure the admin typed-name confirmation is compared in-memory and never written to logs or telemetry, and that consent/erasure event metadata surfaced in copy stays id-free.

- **[low]** "Manage all consent →" link goes to an unspecified surface. `consumer-privacy.html` State A offers "Manage all consent →" but the IA lists "My consent" as the consent surface; the relationship (is the privacy card a preview? does the link deep-link to per-purpose history?) isn't pinned, risking a dead or duplicative destination. *Fix:* state in the spine that the privacy card is a summary and the link targets the full "My consent" surface; ensure withdraw/grant parity exists there too.

## What's already strong

The two-register split (Admin terse / Consumer plain) is clearly stated and the consumer mocks largely live up to it. The erased-state handling is exemplary: tombstone copy with no PII, links that resolve gracefully, color-plus-label everywhere. Eventual-consistency framing ("Showing what we last knew — refreshing…") is calm and non-alarming, and the optimistic-then-reconcile consent pattern is the right UX. The validation-rejection copy ("nothing you typed was lost") and the fail-closed tenant message ("still warming up," not "access denied") show real empathy. The instinct to "name the right" is present throughout — it just needs to be applied honestly to the bases that *aren't* consent, and decoupled from promises about timing the system can't guarantee.
